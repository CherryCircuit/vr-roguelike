# Shared Marketing Skills Reference

This file contains key frameworks, checklists, and mental models extracted from the marketingskills repository. Use these as quick reference when working on marketing tasks.

## Page CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization)

### CRO Analysis Framework (Priority Order)

1. **Value Proposition Clarity** (Highest Impact)
   - Can a visitor understand what this is and why they should care within 5 seconds?
   - Is the primary benefit clear, specific, and differentiated?
   - Is it written in the customer's language, not company jargon?

2. **Headline Effectiveness**
   - Does it communicate the core value proposition?
   - Is it specific enough to be meaningful?
   - Does it match the traffic source's messaging?
   - Strong patterns: Outcome-focused, Specificity, Social proof

3. **CTA Placement, Copy, and Hierarchy**
   - One clear primary action
   - Visible without scrolling
   - Button copy communicates value, not just action
   - Logical primary vs. secondary CTA structure

4. **Visual Hierarchy and Scannability**
   - Scanning gets main message
   - Most important elements visually prominent
   - Enough white space
   - Images support, don't distract

5. **Trust Signals and Social Proof**
   - Customer logos (recognizable ones)
   - Testimonials (specific, attributed, with photos)
   - Case study snippets with real numbers
   - Review scores and counts
   - Security badges (where relevant)

6. **Objection Handling**
   - Price/value concerns
   - "Will this work for my situation?"
   - Implementation difficulty
   - "What if it doesn't work?"

7. **Friction Points**
   - Too many form fields
   - Unclear next steps
   - Confusing navigation
   - Required information that shouldn't be required
   - Mobile experience issues
   - Long load times

### Output Format

- **Quick Wins**: Easy changes with likely immediate impact
- **High-Impact Changes**: Bigger changes requiring more effort but significant improvement
- **Test Ideas**: Hypotheses worth A/B testing
- **Copy Alternatives**: For key elements (headlines, CTAs), provide 2-3 alternatives with rationale

### Page-Specific Frameworks

- **Homepage**: Clear positioning for cold visitors, quick path to conversion, handle both ready and researching
- **Landing Page**: Message match with traffic source, single CTA, complete argument on one page
- **Pricing Page**: Clear plan comparison, recommended plan indication, address plan-selection anxiety
- **Feature Page**: Connect feature to benefit, use cases and examples, clear path to try/buy
- **Blog Post**: Contextual CTAs matching content topic, inline CTAs at natural stopping points

## Competitor Alternatives

### Core Principles

1. **Honesty Builds Trust**: Acknowledge competitor strengths, be accurate about limitations, don't misrepresent
2. **Depth Over Surface**: Go beyond feature checklists, explain why differences matter
3. **Help Them Decide**: Be clear about who you're best for and who competitor is best for
4. **Modular Content Architecture**: Competitor data centralized, updates propagate to all pages

### Page Formats

**Format 1: [Competitor] Alternative (Singular)**
- Search intent: Actively looking to switch
- URL: `/alternatives/[competitor]` or `/[competitor]-alternative`
- Keywords: "[Competitor] alternative", "alternative to [Competitor]"
- Structure: Why alternatives exist, summary of you as alternative, detailed comparison, who should switch, migration path, social proof, CTA

**Format 2: [Competitor] Alternatives (Plural)**
- Search intent: Researching options, earlier in journey
- URL: `/alternatives/[competitor]-alternatives`
- Keywords: "[Competitor] alternatives", "best [Competitor] alternatives"
- Structure: Why alternatives exist, criteria framework, list of alternatives (you first + 4-7 real options), comparison table, detailed breakdown, recommendation by use case, CTA

**Format 3: You vs [Competitor]**
- Search intent: Directly comparing you to competitor
- URL: `/vs/[competitor]` or `/compare/[you]-vs-[competitor]`
- Keywords: "[You] vs [Competitor]"
- Structure: TL;DR summary, comparison table, detailed comparison by category, who each is best for (be honest), customer testimonials, migration support, CTA

**Format 4: [Competitor A] vs [Competitor B]**
- Search intent: Comparing two competitors (not you directly)
- URL: `/compare/[competitor-a]-vs-[competitor-b]`
- Structure: Overview of both, comparison by category, who each is best for, introduce yourself as third option, comparison table (all three), CTA

### Essential Sections

- TL;DR summary (key differences in 2-3 sentences)
- Paragraph comparisons (go beyond tables)
- Feature comparison (describe, list strengths/limitations, bottom line)
- Pricing comparison (tier-by-tier, what's included, hidden costs, total cost for sample size)
- Who it's for (explicit about ideal customer for each)
- Migration section (what transfers, reconfiguration needed, support offered, customer quotes)

### Research Process

For each competitor, gather:
1. Product research (sign up, use it, document features/UX/limitations)
2. Pricing research (current pricing, what's included, hidden costs)
3. Review mining (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius for praise/complaint themes)
4. Customer feedback (talk to customers who switched both directions)
5. Content research (their positioning, comparison pages, changelog)

### Updates

- Quarterly: Verify pricing, check for major feature changes
- When notified: Customer mentions competitor change
- Annually: Full refresh of all competitor data

## Email Sequence

### Core Principles

1. **One Email, One Job**: Each email has one primary purpose, one main CTA
2. **Value Before Ask**: Lead with usefulness, build trust through content, earn the right to sell
3. **Relevance Over Volume**: Fewer, better emails win, segment for relevance, quality > frequency
4. **Clear Path Forward**: Every email moves them somewhere, links do something useful, make next steps obvious

### Sequence Strategy

**Length**:
- Welcome: 3-7 emails
- Lead nurture: 5-10 emails
- Onboarding: 5-10 emails
- Re-engagement: 3-5 emails

**Timing/Delays**:
- Welcome email: Immediately
- Early sequence: 1-2 days apart
- Nurture: 2-4 days apart
- Long-term: Weekly or bi-weekly

**Subject Line Strategy**:
- Clear > Clever
- Specific > Vague
- Benefit or curiosity-driven
- 40-60 characters ideal
- Patterns that work: Question, How-to, Number, Direct, Story tease

**Preview Text**:
- Extends subject line
- 90-140 characters
- Don't repeat subject line
- Complete the thought or add intrigue

### Sequence Types

**Welcome Sequence (Post-Signup)**: 5-7 emails over 12-14 days
1. Welcome + deliver promised value (immediate)
2. Quick win (day 1-2)
3. Story/Why (day 3-4)
4. Social proof (day 5-6)
5. Overcome objection (day 7-8)
6. Core feature highlight (day 9-11)
7. Conversion (day 12-14)

**Lead Nurture Sequence (Pre-Sale)**: 6-8 emails over 2-3 weeks
1. Deliver lead magnet + intro (immediate)
2. Expand on topic (day 2-3)
3. Problem deep-dive (day 4-5)
4. Solution framework (day 6-8)
5. Case study (day 9-11)
6. Differentiation (day 12-14)
7. Objection handler (day 15-18)
8. Direct offer (day 19-21)

**Re-Engagement Sequence**: 3-4 emails over 2 weeks (trigger: 30-60 days inactivity)
1. Check-in (genuine concern)
2. Value reminder (what's new)
3. Incentive (special offer)
4. Last chance (stay or unsubscribe)

**Onboarding Sequence (Product Users)**: 5-7 emails over 14 days
1. Welcome + first step (immediate)
2. Getting started help (day 1)
3. Feature highlight (day 2-3)
4. Success story (day 4-5)
5. Check-in (day 7)
6. Advanced tip (day 10-12)
7. Upgrade/expand (day 14+)

### Email Copy Guidelines

**Structure**: Hook → Context → Value → CTA → Sign-off

**Formatting**: Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences), white space, bullet points, bold sparingly, mobile-first

**Tone**: Conversational (not formal), first/second person, active voice, read aloud—does it sound human?

**Length**: 50-125 words (transactional), 150-300 words (educational), 300-500 words (story-driven)

**CTA Guidelines**: Buttons for primary, links for secondary, one clear primary per email, button text = Action + outcome

## Cold Email

### Writing Principles

1. **Write like a peer, not a vendor**: Read like it came from someone who understands their world, use contractions, read aloud
2. **Every sentence must earn its place**: Ruthlessly short, if it doesn't move reader toward replying, cut it
3. **Personalization must connect to the problem**: Remove personalized opening—if email still makes sense, personalization isn't working
4. **Lead with their world, not yours**: "You/your" should dominate over "I/we"
5. **One ask, low friction**: Interest-based CTAs beat meeting requests, one CTA per email, easy one-line reply

### Voice & Tone

Target voice: Smart colleague who noticed something relevant and is sharing it. Conversational but not sloppy. Confident but not pushy.

Calibrate to audience:
- C-suite: Ultra-brief, peer-level, understated
- Mid-level: More specific value, slightly more detail
- Technical: Precise, no fluff, respect intelligence

What it should NOT sound like: Template with fields swapped, pitch deck compressed into paragraph form, LinkedIn DM from stranger, AI-generated (avoid: "I hope this email finds you well", "I came across your profile", "leverage", "synergy", "best-in-class")

### Structure

Choose a framework that fits the situation:
- Observation → Problem → Proof → Ask
- Question → Value → Ask
- Trigger → Insight → Ask
- Story → Bridge → Ask

### Subject Lines

Short, boring, internal-looking. Job is to get opened, not sell.
- 2-4 words, lowercase, no punctuation tricks
- Look like it came from colleague ("reply rates", "hiring ops", "Q2 forecast")
- No product pitches, no urgency, no emojis, no prospect's first name

### Follow-Up Sequences

- 3-5 total emails, increasing gaps between them
- Each email should stand alone (they may not have read previous ones)
- The breakup email is your last touch—honor it
- Each follow-up should add something new (different angle, fresh proof, useful resource)

## A/B Test Setup

### Core Principles

1. **Start with a Hypothesis**: Not "let's see what happens", specific prediction based on reasoning/data
2. **Test One Thing**: Single variable per test, otherwise don't know what worked
3. **Statistical Rigor**: Pre-determine sample size, don't peek and stop early, commit to methodology
4. **Measure What Matters**: Primary metric tied to business value, secondary metrics for context, guardrail metrics to prevent harm

### Hypothesis Framework

```
Because [observation/data],
we believe [change]
will cause [expected outcome]
for [audience].
We'll know this is true when [metrics].
```

### Test Types

| Type | Description | Traffic Needed |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| A/B | Two versions, single change | Moderate |
| A/B/n | Multiple variants | Higher |
| MVT | Multiple changes in combinations | Very high |
| Split URL | Different URLs for variants | Moderate |

### Sample Size Quick Reference

| Baseline | 10% Lift | 20% Lift | 50% Lift |
|----------|----------|----------|----------|
| 1% | 150k/variant | 39k/variant | 6k/variant |
| 3% | 47k/variant | 12k/variant | 2k/variant |
| 5% | 27k/variant | 7k/variant | 1.2k/variant |
| 10% | 12k/variant | 3k/variant | 550/variant |

### Metrics Selection

- **Primary**: Single metric that matters most, tied to hypothesis, what you'll use to call test
- **Secondary**: Support primary metric interpretation, explain why/how change worked
- **Guardrail**: Things that shouldn't get worse, stop test if significantly negative

### Traffic Allocation

- Standard: 50/50 (default)
- Conservative: 90/10, 80/20 (limit risk of bad variant)
- Ramping: Start small, increase (technical risk mitigation)

### Implementation

- **Client-Side**: JavaScript modifies after load, quick to implement, can cause flicker
- **Server-Side**: Variant determined before render, no flicker, requires dev work

### Running the Test

**Pre-Launch Checklist**: Hypothesis documented, primary metric defined, sample size calculated, variants implemented, tracking verified, QA completed

**DO**: Monitor technical issues, check segment quality, document external factors

**Avoid**: Peek at results and stop early, change variants mid-test, add traffic from new sources

### Analyzing Results

**Checklist**:
1. Reach sample size? (if not, result is preliminary)
2. Statistically significant? (95% confidence = p-value < 0.05)
3. Effect size meaningful? (compare to MDE, project impact)
4. Secondary metrics consistent?
5. Guardrail concerns?
6. Segment differences? (mobile vs desktop, new vs returning)

**Interpreting Results**:
- Significant winner → Implement variant
- Significant loser → Keep control, learn why
- No significant difference → Need more traffic or bolder test
- Mixed signals → Dig deeper, maybe segment

### Growth Experimentation Program

**The Experiment Loop**:
1. Generate hypotheses (data, research, competitors, customer feedback)
2. Prioritize with ICE scoring
3. Design and run test
4. Analyze results with statistical rigor
5. Promote winners to playbook
6. Generate new hypotheses from learnings

**ICE Prioritization**: Score 1-10 on Impact, Confidence, Ease. ICE Score = (I + C + E) / 3

**Experiment Velocity**:
- Experiments launched per month: 4-8
- Win rate: 20-30%
- Average test duration: 2-4 weeks
- Backlog depth: 20+ hypotheses

**The Experiment Playbook**: When a test wins, document pattern with date, hypothesis, sample size, result, guardrails, segment deltas, why it worked/failed, pattern, apply to, status

## Marketing Psychology & Mental Models

### Foundational Thinking Models

- **First Principles**: Break problems to basic truths, build from there. Ask "why" repeatedly.
- **Jobs to Be Done**: People hire products to get a job done. Focus on outcome, not features.
- **Circle of Competence**: Know what you're good at, stay within it. Venture outside only with learning/expert help.
- **Inversion**: Ask "What would guarantee failure?" then avoid those things.
- **Occam's Razor**: Simplest explanation usually correct. Avoid overcomplicating.
- **Pareto Principle (80/20)**: 80% of results from 20% of efforts. Identify and focus on vital few.
- **Local vs Global Optima**: Don't get stuck optimizing the wrong thing. Zoom out before zooming in.
- **Theory of Constraints**: Every system has one bottleneck. Find and fix it before optimizing elsewhere.
- **Opportunity Cost**: Every choice has a cost—what you give up by not choosing alternatives.
- **Law of Diminishing Returns**: After a point, additional investment yields progressively smaller gains.
- **Second-Order Thinking**: Consider immediate effects AND effects of those effects.
- **Map ≠ Territory**: Models represent reality but aren't reality itself. Stay in touch with actual users.
- **Probabilistic Thinking**: Think in probabilities, not certainties. Estimate likelihoods and plan for scenarios.
- **Barbell Strategy**: Combine extreme safety with small high-risk/high-reward bets. Avoid mediocre middle.

### Understanding Buyers

- **Fundamental Attribution Error**: Attribute others' behavior to character, not circumstances. Examine your process before blaming them.
- **Mere Exposure Effect**: People prefer things they've seen before. Familiarity breeds liking.
- **Availability Heuristic**: Judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. Recent/vivid events seem more common.
- **Confirmation Bias**: Seek info confirming existing beliefs, ignore contradictory. Align messaging accordingly.
- **The Lindy Effect**: The longer something survived, the longer likely to continue. Old ideas outlast new ones.
- **Mimetic Desire**: People want things because others want them. Desire is socially contagious.
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy**: Continue investing because of past investment, even when irrational. Know when to kill underperformers.
- **Endowment Effect**: Value things more once you own them. Free trials/samples/freemium work.
- **IKEA Effect**: Value things more when you've put effort into creating them. Let customers customize/configure.
- **Zero-Price Effect**: Free is psychologically different, triggers irrational preference. Free tiers/trials/shipping work.
- **Hyperbolic Discounting / Present Bias**: Prefer immediate rewards over future ones. Emphasize immediate benefits.
- **Status-Quo Bias**: Prefer current state. Change requires effort and feels risky. Reduce friction to switch.
- **Default Effect**: Accept pre-selected options. Defaults are powerful. Pre-select plan you want.
- **Paradox of Choice**: Too many options overwhelm and paralyze. Limit options. Recommend "best for most."
- **Goal-Gradient Effect**: Accelerate effort as approaching goal. Show progress bars, "almost there."
- **Peak-End Rule**: Judge experiences by peak and end, not average. Design memorable peaks and strong endings.
- **Zeigarnik Effect**: Unfinished tasks occupy mind more than completed. "You're 80% done" creates pull.
- **Pratfall Effect**: Competent people more likable when showing small flaw. Admit weakness to increase trust.
- **Curse of Knowledge**: Once you know something, can't imagine not knowing it. Test copy with unfamiliar people.
- **Mental Accounting**: Treat money differently based on source/intended use. Frame in favorable mental accounts.
- **Regret Aversion**: Avoid actions that might cause regret. Address directly with guarantees/trials.
- **Bandwagon Effect / Social Proof**: Follow what others do. Show counts, testimonials, logos, reviews.

### Influencing Behavior

- **Reciprocity Principle**: People feel obligated to return favors. Give first, create reciprocal obligation.
- **Commitment & Consistency**: Once committed, want to stay consistent. Get small commitments first.
- **Authority Bias**: Defer to experts. Feature expert endorsements, certifications, "featured in."
- **Liking / Similarity Bias**: Say yes to those they like/similar to. Use relatable spokespeople, insider language.
- **Unity Principle**: Shared identity drives influence. Position brand as part of customer's tribe.
- **Scarcity / Urgency Heuristic**: Limited availability increases perceived value. Only use when genuine.
- **Foot-in-the-Door**: Start small, escalate. Free trial → paid → annual → enterprise.
- **Door-in-the-Face**: Start unreasonably large, retreat to what you actually want. Contrast makes second seem reasonable.
- **Loss Aversion / Prospect Theory**: Losses feel 2x as painful as equivalent gains feel good. Frame in terms of loss by not acting.
- **Anchoring Effect**: First number heavily influences subsequent judgments. Show higher price first.
- **Decoy Effect**: Adding third, inferior option makes one of original two look better.
- **Framing Effect**: How something's presented changes perception. "90% success" vs "10% failure."
- **Contrast Effect**: Things seem different depending on comparison. Show "before" state clearly.

### Pricing Psychology

- **Charm Pricing / Left-Digit Effect**: Prices ending in 9 seem significantly lower. $99 feels cheaper than $100.
- **Rounded-Price (Fluency) Effect**: Round numbers feel premium. $100 signals quality, $99 signals value.
- **Rule of 100**: Under $100, percentage discounts seem larger. Over $100, absolute discounts seem larger.
- **Price Relativity / Good-Better-Best**: Judge prices relative to options. Three tiers where middle is target.

### Design & Delivery Models

- **Hick's Law**: Decision time increases with number/complexity of choices. Simplify. One clear CTA beats three.
- **AIDA Funnel**: Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. Structure through each stage.
- **Rule of 7**: ~7 touchpoints before converting. Build multi-touch campaigns.
- **Nudge Theory / Choice Architecture**: Small changes in presentation influence decisions. Default selections, strategic ordering.
- **BJ Fogg Behavior Model**: Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt. Design for all three.
- **EAST Framework**: Make behaviors Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely.
- **COM-B Model**: Behavior requires Capability, Opportunity, Motivation. Address all three.
- **Activation Energy**: Initial energy to start. Reduce starting friction.
- **North Star Metric**: One metric capturing value delivered. Align all efforts.
- **The Cobra Effect**: Incentives backfire, produce opposite. Test incentive structures.

### Growth & Scaling Models

- **Feedback Loops**: Output becomes input. Build virtuous cycles.
- **Compounding**: Small, consistent gains accumulate. Start early.
- **Network Effects**: Product becomes more valuable as more use it. Design features that improve with users.
- **Flywheel Effect**: Sustained effort creates momentum. Hard to start, easy to maintain.
- **Switching Costs**: Price of changing to competitor. Increase ethically.
- **Exploration vs Exploitation**: Balance trying new things with optimizing what works.
- **Critical Mass / Tipping Point**: Threshold after which growth self-sustains. Focus on reaching in one segment.
- **Survivorship Bias**: Focusing on successes, ignoring failures. Study failed campaigns.

## Social Content

### Content Pillars Framework

Build content around 3-5 pillars aligned with expertise and audience interests.

**Example SaaS Founder**:
- Industry insights: 30%
- Behind-the-scenes: 25%
- Educational: 25%
- Personal: 15%
- Promotional: 5%

**Pillar Development Questions**:
1. What unique perspective do you have?
2. What questions does your audience ask?
3. What content has performed well?
4. What can you create consistently?
5. What aligns with business goals?

### Hook Formulas

**Curiosity Hooks**:
- "I was wrong about [common belief]."
- "The real reason [outcome] happens isn't what you think."
- "[Impressive result] — and it only took [surprisingly short time]."

**Story Hooks**:
- "Last week, [unexpected thing] happened."
- "I almost [big mistake/failure]."
- "3 years ago, I [past state]. Today, [current state]."

**Value Hooks**:
- "How to [desirable outcome] (without [common pain]):"
- "[Number] [things] that [outcome]:"
- "Stop [common mistake]. Do this instead:"

**Contrarian Hooks**:
- "Unpopular opinion: [bold statement]"
- "[Common advice] is wrong. Here's why:"
- "I stopped [common practice] and [positive result]."

### Content Repurposing System

**Blog Post → Social**:
- LinkedIn: Key insight + link in comments
- LinkedIn: Carousel of main points
- Twitter/X: Thread of takeaways
- Instagram: Carousel with visuals, Reel summarizing

**Podcast/Video → Social**:
Extract content atoms (5-10 per piece):
- Quotable moment (15-60 sec) → Twitter/X, LinkedIn, TikTok
- Story arc (60-90 sec) → Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts
- Tactical tip (30-60 sec) → LinkedIn, YouTube Shorts
- Controversial take → Twitter/X, LinkedIn
- Data/stat callout → LinkedIn carousel, Twitter/X
- Behind-the-scenes → Instagram Stories, TikTok

**Podcast repurposing workflow**:
1. Get transcript
2. Mark timestamps (flag 5-10 best moments)
3. Extract clips
4. Write standalone captions
5. Add subtitles (most video watched without sound)
6. Schedule across 1-2 weeks

**Per episode, aim for**:
- 3-5 short video clips or audiograms (15-60 sec)
- 1-2 LinkedIn text posts
- 1 Twitter/X thread
- 1 carousel summarizing framework/list
- 1 newsletter section or blog post

### Engagement Strategy

**Daily Engagement Routine (30 min)**:
1. Respond to all comments (5 min)
2. Comment on 5-10 posts from target accounts (15 min)
3. Share/repost with added insight (5 min)
4. Send 2-3 DMs to new connections (5 min)

**Quality Comments**: Add new insight, share experience, ask thoughtful question, respectfully disagree with nuance

**Building Relationships**:
- Identify 20-50 accounts in your space
- Consistently engage with their content
- Share their content with credit
- Eventually collaborate

### Analytics & Optimization

**Metrics That Matter**:
- Awareness: Impressions, Reach, Follower growth rate
- Engagement: Engagement rate, Comments (higher value than likes), Shares/reposts, Saves
- Conversion: Link clicks, Profile visits, DMs received, Leads attributed

**Weekly Review**: Top 3 performing posts (why worked), bottom 3 (what learn), follower growth trend, engagement rate trend, best posting times

## Sales Enablement

### Core Principles

1. **Sales Uses What Sales Trusts**: Involve reps in creation, use their language, test with top performers
2. **Situation-Specific, Not Generic**: Tailor to persona, deal stage, use case
3. **Scannable Over Comprehensive**: Reps need information in 3 seconds, not 30
4. **Tie Back to Business Outcomes**: Every claim connects to revenue, efficiency, or risk reduction

### Sales Deck / Pitch Deck (10-12 Slides)

1. Current World Problem — The pain your buyer lives with today
2. Cost of the Problem — What inaction costs (time, money, risk)
3. The Shift Happening — Market or technology change creating urgency
4. Your Approach — How you solve it differently
5. Product Walkthrough — 3-4 key workflows, not feature tour
6. Proof Points — Metrics, logos, analyst recognition
7. Case Study — One customer story told well
8. Implementation / Timeline — How they get from here to live
9. ROI / Value — Expected return and payback period
10. Pricing Overview — Transparent, tiered if applicable
11. Next Steps / CTA — Clear action with timeline

**Deck Principles**:
- Story arc, not feature tour
- One idea per slide
- Design for presenting, not reading (minimal text, strong visuals)

**Customization by Buyer Type**:
- Technical buyer: Emphasize architecture, security, integrations, API; de-emphasize ROI, business metrics
- Economic buyer: Emphasize ROI, payback period, total cost, risk; de-emphasize technical details
- Champion: Emphasize internal selling points, quick wins, peer proof; de-emphasize deep detail

### One-Pagers / Leave-Behinds

**Structure**:
1. Problem statement (one sentence)
2. Your solution (what you do and how)
3. 3 differentiators (why you vs alternatives)
4. Proof point (one strong metric or customer quote)
5. CTA (clear next step with contact info)

**Design Principles**: One page literally, scannable in 30 seconds, bold headers/short bullets/whitespace, include logo/website/specific contact, match brand but keep clean

### Objection Handling Docs

**Categories**:
- Price: "Too expensive", "No budget", "Competitor is cheaper"
- Timing: "Not right time", "Maybe next quarter", "Too busy"
- Competition: "Already use X", "What makes you different?"
- Authority: "Need to check with boss", "Committee decides"
- Status quo: "What we have works fine", "Not broken, don't fix"
- Technical: "Integrate with X?", "Security concerns", "Can it scale?"

**Response Framework**:
1. Objection statement (exactly how reps hear it)
2. Why they say it (real concern behind words)
3. Response approach (acknowledge and redirect)
4. Proof point (specific evidence)
5. Follow-up question (keep conversation moving)

**Two Formats**: Quick-reference table for live calls, detailed doc for prep and training

### ROI Calculators & Value Props

**Inputs** (current state metrics): Time spent on manual processes, current tool costs, error rates, team size

**Calculations** (your formula): Time saved, cost reduction, revenue impact

**Outputs** (what prospect sees): Annual ROI %, Payback period in months, Total 3-year value

**Value Prop by Persona**:
- CTO/VP Eng: Architecture, scale, security, team velocity → Technical superiority, integration depth
- VP Sales: Pipeline, quota attainment, rep productivity → Revenue impact, time savings
- CFO: Total cost, payback period, risk → ROI, cost reduction, predictability
- End user: Ease of use, daily workflow, learning curve → Time saved, frustration eliminated

### Demo Scripts & Talk Tracks

**Script Structure**:
1. Opening (2 min) — Context, agenda, confirm goals
2. Discovery recap (3 min) — Summarize what learned, confirm priorities
3. Solution walkthrough (15-20 min) — 3-4 key workflows mapped to their pain
4. Interaction points — Questions during demo, not just at end
5. Close (5 min) — Summarize value, propose next steps with timeline

**Talk Track Types**:
- Discovery call: 30 min (qualify, understand pain, map buying process)
- First demo: 30-45 min (show 3-4 workflows tied to their pain)
- Technical deep-dive: 45-60 min (architecture, security, integrations, API)
- Executive overview: 20-30 min (business outcomes, ROI, strategic alignment)

### Case Study Briefs (Sales Format)

**Structure**:
1. Customer profile (industry, company size, buyer role)
2. Challenge (what struggling with, 2-3 sentences)
3. Solution (what implemented, 1-2 sentences)
4. Results (3 specific metrics, before/after)
5. Pull quote (one sentence from customer)
6. Tags (industry, use case, company size, persona)

### Sales Playbooks

**What Goes In**:
- Buyer profile (who selling to, goals and pains)
- Qualification criteria (BANT, MEDDIC, or your framework)
- Discovery questions (organized by topic, not script)
- Objection handling (top 10 objections with responses)
- Competitive positioning (how you win against each)
- Demo flow (recommended sequence for each persona)
- Email templates (follow-up, proposal, check-in, breakup)

**When to Build**: New product launch, new market segment, new hire ramp

**Keeping It Living**: Review quarterly, get input from top reps, remove outdated content, assign owner

## Copy Editing

### The Seven Sweeps Framework

Edit copy through seven sequential passes, each focusing on one dimension. After each sweep, loop back to check previous sweeps.

**Sweep 1: Clarity**
Focus: Can reader understand what you're saying?
Check: Confusing sentences, unclear pronouns, jargon, ambiguous statements, missing context
After: Confirm "Rule of One" (one main idea per section) and "You Rule" (copy speaks to reader)

**Sweep 2: Voice and Tone**
Focus: Is copy consistent in how it sounds?
Check: Shifts between formal/casual, inconsistent brand personality, jarring mood changes, word choices not matching brand
After: Return to Clarity (voice edits didn't introduce confusion)

**Sweep 3: So What**
Focus: Does every claim answer "why should I care?"
Check: Features without benefits, claims without consequences, statements not connecting to reader's life, missing "which means..." bridges
The test: For every statement, ask "So what?" If copy doesn't answer with deeper benefit, needs work
After: Return to Voice, then Clarity

**Sweep 4: Prove It**
Focus: Is every claim supported with evidence?
Check: Unsubstantiated claims, missing social proof, assertions without backup, "best"/"leading" without evidence
Types of proof: Testimonials with names/specifics, case study references, stats/data, third-party validation, guarantees, customer logos, review scores
After: Return to So What, Voice, then Clarity

**Sweep 5: Specificity**
Focus: Is copy concrete enough to be compelling?
Check: Vague language ("improve", "enhance", "optimize"), generic statements, round numbers that feel made up, missing details
After: Return to Prove It, So What, Voice, then Clarity

**Sweep 6: Heightened Emotion**
Focus: Does copy make reader feel something?
Check: Flat, informational language, missing emotional triggers, pain points mentioned but not felt, aspirations stated but not evoked
Emotional dimensions: Pain of current state, frustration with alternatives, fear of missing out, desire for transformation, pride in smart choices, relief from solving problem
After: Return to Specificity, Prove It, So What, Voice, then Clarity

**Sweep 7: Zero Risk**
Focus: Have we removed every barrier to action?
Check: Friction near CTAs, unanswered objections, missing trust signals, unclear next steps, hidden costs
Risk reducers: Money-back guarantees, free trials, "no credit card required", "cancel anytime", social proof near CTA, clear expectations, privacy assurances
After: Return through all previous sweeps one final time: Heightened Emotion, Specificity, Prove It, So What, Voice, Clarity

### Quick-Pass Editing Checks

**Cut these words**: Very, really, extremely, incredibly; Just, actually, basically; In order to (use "to"); That (often unnecessary); Things, stuff

**Replace these**:
- Utilize → Use
- Implement → Set up
- Leverage → Use
- Facilitate → Help
- Innovative → New
- Robust → Strong
- Seamless → Smooth
- Cutting-edge → New/Modern

**Watch for**: Adverbs (usually unnecessary), passive voice (switch to active), nominalizations (verb→noun)

**Sentence-level**: One idea per sentence, vary length, front-load important info, max 3 conjunctions, no more than 25 words

**Paragraph-level**: One topic per paragraph, short paragraphs (2-4 sentences for web), strong openings, logical flow, white space

### Common Copy Problems & Fixes

- Wall of features → Add "which means..." after each feature
- Corporate speak → Ask "How would a human say this?"
- Weak opening → Lead with reader's problem or desired outcome
- Buried CTA → Make CTA obvious, early, and repeated
- No proof → Add specific testimonials, numbers, or case references
- Generic claims → Specify who, how, and by how much
- Mixed audiences → Pick one audience and write directly to them
- Feature overload → Focus on 3-5 key benefits

## Launch Strategy

### The ORB Framework

Structure launch marketing across three channel types. Everything leads back to owned channels.

**Owned Channels** (you own channel, not audience):
- Examples: Email list, blog, podcast, branded community (Slack/Discord), website/product
- Why: Get more effective over time, no algorithm changes, direct relationship, compound value
- Start with 1-2 based on audience

**Rented Channels** (platforms providing visibility but no control):
- Examples: Social media (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Instagram), app stores, YouTube, Reddit
- How: Pick 1-2 where audience active, use to drive traffic to owned channels

**Borrowed Channels** (tap into someone else's audience):
- Examples: Guest content, collaborations, speaking engagements, influencer partnerships
- Be proactive: List industry leaders your audience follows, pitch win-win collaborations

### Five-Phase Launch Approach

**Phase 1: Internal Launch** (gather feedback, iron out issues)
- Recruit early users one-on-one to test for free
- Collect feedback on usability gaps and missing features
- Ensure prototype functional enough to demo

**Phase 2: Alpha Launch** (external users in controlled way)
- Create landing page with early access signup form
- Announce product exists
- Invite users individually to start testing
- MVP working in production (even if still evolving)

**Phase 3: Beta Launch** (scale up early access, generate buzz)
- Work through early access list (some free, some paid)
- Start marketing with teasers about problems you solve
- Recruit friends, investors, and influencers to test and share

**Phase 4: Early Access Launch** (shift from testing to controlled expansion)
- Leak product details: screenshots, feature GIFs, demos
- Gather quantitative usage data and qualitative feedback
- Run user research with engaged users (incentivize)
- Expansion: Throttle invites in batches (5-10% at a time) OR invite all at once

**Phase 5: Full Launch** (open floodgates)
- Open self-serve signups
- Start charging (if not already)
- Announce general availability across all channels
- Launch touchpoints: Customer emails, in-app popups/tours, website banner, "New" sticker, blog post, social posts, Product Hunt/BetaList/Hacker News

### Post-Launch Product Marketing

**Immediate Actions**:
- Set up automated onboarding email sequence
- Include announcement in weekly/biweekly/monthly roundup
- Publish comparison pages highlighting why you're obvious choice
- Add dedicated sections about new feature/product across your site
- Create no-code interactive demo (Navattic, etc.)

**Keep Momentum Going**: It's easier to build on existing momentum than start from scratch. Every touchpoint reinforces launch.

### Ongoing Launch Strategy

**Prioritization Matrix**:
- Major updates (new features, overhauls): Full campaign across multiple channels
- Medium updates (integrations, UI enhancements): Targeted announcement, email to relevant segments, in-app banner
- Minor updates (bug fixes, small tweaks): Changelog and release notes

**Announcement Tactics**:
- Space out releases: Stagger announcements to maintain momentum
- Reuse high-performing tactics: Apply insights from previous announcements
- Keep engaging: Continue using email, social, in-app messaging
- Signal active development: Even small changelog updates remind customers product is evolving

## SEO Audit

### Audit Framework (Priority Order)

1. **Crawlability & Indexation** (can Google find and index it?)
2. **Technical Foundations** (is site fast and functional?)
3. **On-Page Optimization** (is content optimized?)
4. **Content Quality** (does it deserve to rank?)
5. **Authority & Links** (does it have credibility?)

### Technical SEO Audit

**Crawlability**:
- Robots.txt (check for unintentional blocks, verify important pages allowed, check sitemap reference)
- XML Sitemap (exists and accessible, submitted to Search Console, contains only canonical/indexable URLs, updated regularly)
- Site Architecture (important pages within 3 clicks, logical hierarchy, internal linking, no orphan pages)
- Crawl Budget (for large sites: parameterized URLs under control, faceted navigation handled, infinite scroll with pagination, no session IDs in URLs)

**Indexation**:
- Index Status (site:domain.com check, Search Console coverage report, compare indexed vs expected)
- Indexation Issues (noindex tags on important pages, canonicals wrong direction, redirect chains/loops, soft 404s, duplicate content without canonicals)
- Canonicalization (all pages have canonical tags, self-referencing on unique pages, HTTP→HTTPS canonicals, www vs non-www consistency, trailing slash consistency)

**Site Speed & Core Web Vitals**:
- LCP < 2.5s, INP < 200ms, CLS < 0.1
- Factors: Server response time, image optimization, JavaScript execution, CSS delivery, caching headers, CDN, font loading
- Tools: PageSpeed Insights, WebPageTest, Chrome DevTools, Search Console Core Web Vitals report

**Mobile-Friendliness**:
- Responsive design (not separate m.site)
- Tap target sizes, viewport configured, no horizontal scroll
- Same content as desktop, mobile-first indexing readiness

**Security & HTTPS**:
- HTTPS across entire site, valid SSL certificate, no mixed content, HTTP→HTTPS redirects, HSTS header

**URL Structure**:
- Readable, descriptive URLs, keywords in URLs where natural, consistent structure, no unnecessary parameters, lowercase and hyphen-separated

### On-Page SEO Audit

**Title Tags**:
- Unique titles per page, primary keyword near beginning, 50-60 characters, compelling and click-worthy, no brand name placement

**Meta Descriptions**:
- Unique per page, 150-160 characters, includes primary keyword, clear value proposition, call to action

**Heading Structure**:
- One H1 per page, H1 contains primary keyword, logical hierarchy (H1→H2→H3), headings describe content, not just styling

**Content Optimization**:
- Keyword in first 100 words, related keywords naturally used, sufficient depth/length for topic, answers search intent, better than competitors
- Thin content issues: pages with little unique content, tag/category pages with no value, doorway pages, duplicate/near-duplicate content

**Image Optimization**:
- Descriptive file names, alt text on all images, alt text describes image, compressed file sizes, modern formats (WebP), lazy loading, responsive images

**Internal Linking**:
- Important pages well-linked, descriptive anchor text, logical relationships, no broken links, reasonable link count per page

**Keyword Targeting**:
- Per page: Clear primary keyword, title/H1/URL aligned, content satisfies intent, not competing with other pages
- Site-wide: Keyword mapping document, no major gaps, no cannibalization, logical topical clusters

### Content Quality Assessment

**E-E-A-T Signals**:
- Experience: First-hand experience demonstrated, original insights/data, real examples and case studies
- Expertise: Author credentials visible, accurate detailed info, properly sourced claims
- Authoritativeness: Recognized in space, cited by others, industry credentials
- Trustworthiness: Accurate information, transparent about business, contact available, privacy policy/terms, secure site

**Content Depth**: Comprehensive coverage, answers follow-up questions, better than top-ranking competitors, updated and current

**User Engagement Signals**: Time on page, bounce rate in context, pages per session, return visits

### Output Format

**Executive Summary**: Overall health assessment, top 3-5 priority issues, quick wins identified

**Technical SEO Findings**: For each issue — Issue, Impact (High/Medium/Low), Evidence, Fix, Priority

**On-Page SEO Findings**: Same format

**Content Findings**: Same format

**Prioritized Action Plan**:
1. Critical fixes (blocking indexation/ranking)
2. High-impact improvements
3. Quick wins (easy, immediate benefit)
4. Long-term recommendations
